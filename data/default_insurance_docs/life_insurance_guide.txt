LIFE INSURANCE - COMPLETE GUIDE

================================================================================
SECTION 1: LIFE INSURANCE FUNDAMENTALS
================================================================================

What is Life Insurance?

Life insurance is a contract between you and an insurance company. You pay premiums, and the insurance company pays a lump sum (death benefit) to your beneficiaries when you die. It provides financial protection for your loved ones.

Why You Need It:

Financial Protection:
- Replace your income for dependents
- Pay off mortgage and debts
- Cover funeral expenses ($7,000-$12,000 average)
- Fund children's education
- Maintain family's standard of living
- Cover estate taxes
- Leave inheritance

Who Needs Life Insurance?

High Priority:
- Parents with minor children
- Primary breadwinners
- Homeowners with mortgages
- Business owners with partners
- Anyone with significant debt
- High-net-worth individuals (estate planning)

Lower Priority:
- Single individuals without dependents
- Retirees with sufficient assets
- Children (usually unnecessary)
- Stay-at-home parents (but consider childcare replacement value)

How Much Coverage Do You Need?

Common Rules of Thumb:

1. Income Replacement Method:
   10-15 times your annual income
   Example: $50,000 salary = $500,000-$750,000 coverage

2. DIME Formula:
   Debt + Income (10x) + Mortgage + Education
   Example:
   - Debt: $20,000 (credit cards, car loans)
   - Income: $500,000 (10x $50,000)
   - Mortgage: $200,000 (remaining balance)
   - Education: $100,000 (2 children, $50,000 each)
   - Total: $820,000 coverage needed

3. Human Life Value Method:
   Calculate present value of future earnings
   More complex but most accurate

Average Coverage Amounts:
- Individual: $150,000-$400,000
- Primary breadwinner: $500,000-$1,000,000
- High earners: $1,000,000-$5,000,000+

Consider Inflation:
$500,000 today may only have $350,000 purchasing power in 20 years (assuming 3% inflation).

SECTION 2: TERM LIFE INSURANCE
================================================================================

What is Term Life Insurance?

Coverage for specific period (term). If you die during term, beneficiaries receive death benefit. If you outlive term, coverage ends (no payout). Most affordable life insurance type.

Term Lengths:
- 10-year term
- 15-year term
- 20-year term (most popular)
- 30-year term

How it Works:

You choose:
- Coverage amount ($250,000, $500,000, $1,000,000, etc.)
- Term length (10, 15, 20, 30 years)
- Level premiums (same cost throughout term)

After term ends:
- Coverage expires
- Can renew (higher rates)
- Can convert to permanent policy
- Can purchase new term policy (subject to health review)

Cost Examples (Healthy 35-year-old):

$500,000, 20-year term:
- Male: $25-$35/month
- Female: $20-$30/month

$1,000,000, 20-year term:
- Male: $45-$60/month
- Female: $35-$50/month

Cost Examples (Healthy 45-year-old):

$500,000, 20-year term:
- Male: $60-$80/month
- Female: $50-$65/month

Pros:
- Most affordable
- Simple to understand
- High coverage amounts available
- Covers temporary needs (mortgage, children)

Cons:
- No cash value
- Coverage ends after term
- Renewal rates very expensive
- No return if you outlive term

Best For:
- Young families
- Mortgage protection
- Income replacement during working years
- Temporary financial obligations
- Budget-conscious buyers

Types of Term Insurance:

Level Term:
- Death benefit stays same
- Premiums stay same
- Most common type

Decreasing Term:
- Death benefit decreases over time
- Premiums stay same
- Often used for mortgage protection
- Cheaper than level term

Increasing Term:
- Death benefit increases over time
- Premiums increase
- Rare option

Return of Premium (ROP):
- If you outlive term, premiums refunded
- 2-3x more expensive
- Rarely worth the cost

Convertible Term:
- Can convert to permanent policy without medical exam
- Usually within first 10-20 years
- Valuable feature if health declines

Annual Renewable Term:
- One-year coverage
- Renew annually
- Premiums increase each year
- Most expensive long-term

SECTION 3: WHOLE LIFE INSURANCE
================================================================================

What is Whole Life Insurance?

Permanent life insurance providing lifelong coverage. Combines death benefit with cash value savings component. Premiums stay level for life. Part of premium goes to insurance cost, part builds cash value.

How Cash Value Works:

Year 1-3:
- Minimal cash value (fees and commissions)
- Surrender charges high
- Not accessible

Year 5-10:
- Cash value slowly growing (2-4% annually)
- Can borrow against or withdraw
- Still significant surrender charges

Year 20+:
- Substantial cash value accumulated
- Tax-deferred growth
- Can access via loans or withdrawals

Guaranteed Values:
- Death benefit guaranteed
- Cash value growth guaranteed (typically 2-3% annually)
- Premiums fixed for life

Dividends (Participating Policies):
- Some mutual insurance companies pay dividends
- Not guaranteed (but historically consistent)
- Can be used to:
  * Buy additional coverage
  * Reduce premiums
  * Accumulate with interest
  * Receive as cash
- Historical dividend rates: 5-7% (but declining recently)

Cost Examples (Healthy 35-year-old):

$250,000 whole life:
- Male: $200-$300/month
- Female: $180-$250/month

$500,000 whole life:
- Male: $400-$600/month
- Female: $350-$500/month

Compare to Term:
- Whole life: 8-12x more expensive than term
- Example: $500K coverage
  * 20-year term: $40/month
  * Whole life: $400/month

Pros:
- Lifelong coverage (won't outlive it)
- Level premiums (never increase)
- Cash value accumulation
- Tax-deferred growth
- Forced savings
- Can borrow against cash value
- Guaranteed death benefit

Cons:
- Very expensive (8-12x term cost)
- Low early cash value
- Cash value growth slow (2-4%)
- Complex fees
- Long break-even period (15-20 years)
- Better investment alternatives exist
- Surrender charges if cancelled early

Best For:
- High-net-worth individuals (estate planning)
- Lifelong dependents (special needs)
- Business succession planning
- Those maximizing retirement accounts already
- People wanting forced savings
- Estate liquidity needs

Not Ideal For:
- Young families on tight budget
- Temporary insurance needs
- People seeking investment returns
- Those with limited income

Cash Value Access:

Policy Loans:
- Borrow against cash value
- Interest charged (4-8%)
- No credit check required
- Reduces death benefit if unpaid
- Flexible repayment

Withdrawals:
- Take money directly from cash value
- Up to basis (premiums paid) usually tax-free
- Reduces cash value and death benefit
- Permanent reduction

Surrender:
- Cancel policy and receive cash value
- Surrender charges typically first 10-15 years
- Gains above premiums paid are taxable
- Lose death benefit coverage

SECTION 4: UNIVERSAL LIFE INSURANCE
================================================================================

What is Universal Life (UL)?

Permanent life insurance with flexible premiums and death benefit. Like whole life but with adjustable features. Cash value earns interest based on market rates.

Key Features:

Flexible Premiums:
- Pay more or less (within limits)
- Skip payments if cash value sufficient
- Adjust to changing finances

Adjustable Death Benefit:
- Increase coverage (subject to underwriting)
- Decrease coverage
- Choose between Level or Increasing death benefit

Transparency:
- See cost of insurance charges
- See cash value credits
- More transparent than whole life

Interest Crediting:
- Minimum guaranteed rate (1-2%)
- Current rate tied to market (3-5% recently)
- Rates can fluctuate
- Credited monthly or annually

Types of Universal Life:

Standard Universal Life:
- Cash value earns interest based on insurer's portfolio
- Typically tied to bond yields
- Current rates: 3-5%
- Minimum guaranteed: 1-2%

Indexed Universal Life (IUL):
- Cash value tied to stock market index (S&P 500)
- Participation in market gains (50-100% of index)
- Floor protection (0% minimum, won't lose money)
- Cap on gains (8-12% typically)
- More growth potential than standard UL
- Popular in recent years

Variable Universal Life (VUL):
- Cash value in investment subaccounts (stocks, bonds, funds)
- Full market exposure (can lose money)
- Higher growth potential
- Higher risk
- Requires securities license to sell

Guaranteed Universal Life (GUL):
- Minimum premiums
- No/minimal cash value
- Guarantees death benefit to specific age or lifetime
- More affordable than whole life
- Bridge between term and permanent
- Cannot skip premiums

Cost Examples (Healthy 45-year-old):

$500,000 Universal Life:
- Standard UL: $300-$450/month
- Indexed UL: $350-$500/month
- Guaranteed UL: $200-$300/month

Pros:
- Flexibility in premiums
- Adjustable death benefit
- Transparent costs
- Potential for good returns (IUL)
- Cheaper than whole life
- Cash value access

Cons:
- Complex to understand
- Risk of policy lapse if underfunded
- Interest rates not guaranteed (standard UL)
- Fees can be high
- Requires active management
- Market risk (VUL)

Best For:
- Those wanting flexibility
- Business owners with fluctuating income
- Estate planning with customization needs
- Savvy consumers comfortable with complexity

Risks:

Underfunding:
- If premiums too low + poor performance = policy lapses
- Must monitor cash value regularly
- May need to increase premiums

Interest Rate Risk:
- If rates drop, may need to pay more
- Policy illustrations assume rates that may not materialize

Fees:
- Cost of insurance (increases with age)
- Administrative fees
- Surrender charges
- Fund management fees (VUL)

SECTION 5: THE LIFE INSURANCE APPLICATION PROCESS
================================================================================

Step-by-Step Process:

1. Research and Compare (1-2 weeks):
   - Determine coverage needs
   - Compare quotes from multiple insurers
   - Choose term vs permanent
   - Select death benefit amount

2. Application Submission (1-2 hours):
   - Complete application (online or with agent)
   - Provide personal information
   - Answer medical questions
   - Sign authorization forms

3. Medical Exam (If Required):
   - Paramedical examiner visits (home or office)
   - Blood draw
   - Urine sample
   - Blood pressure, height, weight
   - Medical history review
   - Usually free, insurer pays

4. Medical Records Review (2-4 weeks):
   - Insurer requests records from doctors
   - Reviews prescription history
   - Checks driving record
   - Reviews financial information (large policies)

5. Underwriting (2-6 weeks):
   - Underwriter reviews all information
   - Assigns risk class
   - Determines premium
   - May request additional information

6. Offer Decision:
   - Approved as applied
   - Approved at different rate class (higher premium)
   - Approved with exclusions or riders
   - Postponed (more information needed)
   - Declined

7. Policy Delivery (1 week):
   - Review policy documents
   - Sign delivery receipt
   - Coverage begins
   - Free look period starts (10-30 days)

Total Timeline: 4-8 weeks (average 6 weeks)

Accelerated Underwriting:
- No medical exam
- Automated decision
- Approval in hours/days
- Available for healthy applicants
- Coverage limits (usually up to $500K-$1M)
- Slightly higher premiums

Medical Exam Preparation:

Week Before:
- Avoid alcohol (48 hours before)
- Avoid fatty foods (24 hours before)
- Avoid caffeine (4 hours before)
- Stay hydrated
- Get good sleep
- Avoid vigorous exercise (24 hours before)

Day of Exam:
- Fast (8-12 hours, water okay)
- Morning appointment (better results)
- Relax (stress affects blood pressure)
- Bring ID and medication list

What They Test:

Blood Tests:
- Cholesterol levels (LDL, HDL, triglycerides)
- Blood sugar (diabetes check)
- Liver enzymes
- Kidney function
- Protein levels
- Drug screening
- HIV/AIDS
- Hepatitis
- Cotinine (nicotine)

Urine Tests:
- Kidney function
- Diabetes markers
- Drug screening
- Nicotine/cotinine

Measurements:
- Height and weight (BMI calculation)
- Blood pressure
- Pulse

SECTION 6: UNDERWRITING AND RATE CLASSES
================================================================================

Health Classifications:

Preferred Plus / Super Preferred:
- Excellent health
- Ideal height/weight
- No tobacco (5+ years)
- No chronic conditions
- Clean driving record
- Family history clean
- Best rates (20-30% cheaper than standard)

Preferred:
- Very good health
- Minor issues acceptable
- No tobacco
- Good rates (10-20% cheaper than standard)

Standard Plus:
- Good health
- Some minor health issues
- Controlled conditions acceptable
- Average rates

Standard:
- Average health
- Some health issues
- May have controlled chronic conditions
- May be overweight
- Base rates

Substandard / Table Ratings:
- Significant health issues
- Chronic conditions
- High-risk occupations or hobbies
- Poor driving record
- Rates increase 25-500%+
- Table ratings: A (+25%), B (+50%), C (+75%), D (+100%), etc.

Factors Affecting Rate Class:

Age:
- Younger = cheaper
- Premiums double roughly every 10 years

Gender:
- Women typically 10-20% cheaper (longer life expectancy)

Tobacco Use:
- Smokers pay 2-4x more
- Includes cigarettes, cigars, chewing tobacco, vaping
- Must be tobacco-free 12+ months (most insurers)

Health Conditions:

Well-Controlled (Preferred possible):
- High blood pressure (controlled with medication)
- High cholesterol (controlled)
- Hypothyroidism (controlled)

May Affect Rating:
- Diabetes (Type 2, well-controlled)
- Asthma (moderate)
- Sleep apnea (treated with CPAP)
- Anxiety/depression (stable, no hospitalizations)

Substandard Likely:
- Heart disease
- Cancer (recent or aggressive)
- Stroke
- Diabetes (poorly controlled)
- Obesity (BMI 40+)
- Kidney disease
- COPD

May Decline:
- Cancer (current or recent aggressive)
- Heart attack (recent)
- Cirrhosis
- AIDS
- Serious mental illness with hospitalizations

BMI Impact:

Preferred Plus: 19-26 BMI
Preferred: 26-30 BMI
Standard: 30-35 BMI
Substandard: 35+ BMI

Example: 5'10" male
- Preferred: 140-205 lbs
- Standard: 205-245 lbs
- Substandard: 245+ lbs

Driving Record:

Clean Record: No impact
1 Minor Violation (speeding): Minimal impact
DUI (within 5 years): Substandard or decline
Multiple violations: Substandard
Reckless driving: Substandard

Occupation:

Low Risk (Office Work): No impact
Moderate Risk (Construction): May add premium
High Risk (Logging, fishing, pilots): Table rating or decline

Hobbies:

Low Risk (Jogging, golf): No impact
Moderate Risk (Recreational aviation): Rating
High Risk (Skydiving, rock climbing, scuba): Rating or exclusion

Family History:

Cancer or heart disease in parents before age 60: May affect rating (less impact if in 70s+)

SECTION 7: BENEFICIARIES AND POLICY OWNERSHIP
================================================================================

Beneficiary Basics:

Primary Beneficiary:
- First in line to receive death benefit
- Can name multiple (specify percentages)
- Example: Spouse 100% or Spouse 50%, Child 25%, Child 25%

Contingent Beneficiary (Secondary):
- Receives benefit if primary deceased
- Backup plan
- Important for estate planning

Tertiary Beneficiary:
- Third level backup
- Rarely used

Per Stirpes vs Per Capita:

Per Stirpes ("by branch"):
- If beneficiary dies, their share goes to their descendants
- Example: 3 children (33% each). One child predeceases you. That child's 33% goes to their children.

Per Capita ("by head"):
- If beneficiary dies, their share divided equally among remaining beneficiaries
- Example: 3 children (33% each). One child predeceases you. Remaining 2 children each get 50%.

Naming Beneficiaries:

Specific Individuals:
- Use full legal name
- Include relationship
- Include SSN or date of birth
- Example: "Jane Marie Smith, wife, DOB 01/15/1985"

Trust:
- If minor children, consider trust
- Provides asset management
- Example: "The Smith Family Trust dated January 15, 2025"

Estate:
- Generally avoid (triggers probate)
- Delays distribution
- Subject to creditors
- Use only if no other option

Avoid "My children" - Ambiguous if you have more children later

Updating Beneficiaries:

Review Every 3-5 Years or After:
- Marriage
- Divorce (ex-spouse may still receive benefit!)
- Birth/adoption
- Death of beneficiary
- Significant financial changes
- Moving to new state

Common Mistake: Forgetting to update after divorce. Ex-spouse receives benefit unless you update.

Policy Ownership:

Owner Rights:
- Name/change beneficiaries
- Borrow against cash value
- Change coverage amount
- Cancel policy
- Transfer ownership

Owner Types:

Individual Ownership:
- You own policy on your life
- Most common
- Full control

Cross-Ownership:
- Spouse owns policy on your life
- Can avoid estate taxes
- Used in estate planning

Third-Party Ownership:
- Trust or business owns policy
- Estate planning strategy
- Business continuation planning

Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT):
- Trust owns policy
- Removes from taxable estate
- Cannot undo without beneficiary consent
- Complex but effective for large estates

Business Ownership:
- Company owns policy on key employee
- Key person insurance
- Business continuation

Insurable Interest:

Required Relationship:
- Yourself
- Spouse
- Children
- Business partners
- Key employees
- Anyone you have financial dependency on

Cannot insure:
- Random strangers
- Distant relatives without financial relationship
- Neighbors or friends (usually)

SECTION 8: RIDERS AND POLICY ENHANCEMENTS
================================================================================

Common Riders (Policy Add-Ons):

Waiver of Premium Rider:
- If disabled, insurer waives premiums
- Policy continues in force
- Typically requires 6-month waiting period
- Disability must meet specific definition
- Cost: $20-$50/year per $100K coverage
- Highly recommended

Accelerated Death Benefit Rider:
- Access death benefit if terminally ill (12-24 months to live)
- Receive 25-100% of benefit early
- Use for medical costs, end-of-life care
- Often included free
- Highly recommended

Accidental Death Benefit Rider (Double Indemnity):
- Pays additional benefit if death by accident
- Typically doubles death benefit
- Cost: $5-$15/year per $100K
- Not recommended (death is death, cause shouldn't matter)

Term Rider for Spouse/Children:
- Add term coverage for family members
- Cheaper than separate policies
- Convenient
- Cost: $50-$150/year
- Consider if budget tight

Guaranteed Insurability Rider:
- Buy more coverage later without medical exam
- Useful if expect income increase
- Typically allows purchases at specific ages (25, 28, 31, 34, 37, 40) or life events (marriage, birth)
- Cost: $25-$75/year per $100K base
- Good for young buyers

Conversion Rider:
- Convert term to permanent without exam
- Usually included with term policies
- Valuable if health declines
- Often free

Long-Term Care Rider:
- Access death benefit for long-term care costs
- Accelerates death benefit
- 2-4% of death benefit monthly (typically)
- Reduces death benefit dollar-for-dollar
- Cost: Adds 20-40% to premium
- Consider for estate planning

Chronic Illness Rider:
- Access death benefit if chronically ill (unable to perform 2+ activities of daily living)
- Similar to long-term care rider
- Often included free or low cost
- Recommended

Return of Premium (Term Insurance):
- Refunds premiums if you outlive term
- Doubles or triples premium cost
- Rarely worthwhile
- Not recommended

Cost of Living Adjustment (COLA) Rider:
- Death benefit increases with inflation
- Premiums increase accordingly
- Good for long-term protection
- Cost: Adds 15-25% to premium

Riders to Avoid:

Accidental Death Rider:
- Redundant (death benefit pays regardless)
- Only pays in limited scenarios
- Better to increase base coverage

Return of Premium:
- Expensive (2-3x premium)
- Invest the difference instead
- Better returns elsewhere

SECTION 9: LIVING BENEFITS AND POLICY USES
================================================================================

Using Life Insurance While Living:

Cash Value Loans (Permanent Policies):
- Borrow against cash value
- No credit check
- Interest charged (4-8%)
- Doesn't show on credit report
- Flexible repayment
- Reduces death benefit if unpaid

Uses:
- Emergency funds
- Down payment on home
- Business investment
- Supplement retirement income

Policy Surrender:
- Cancel policy, receive cash value
- Surrender charges may apply (first 10-15 years)
- Gains taxable
- Lose death benefit

Life Settlements:
- Sell policy to third party
- Typically 20-60% of death benefit
- For seniors no longer needing coverage
- Third party becomes beneficiary
- Usually ages 65-85
- May affect Medicaid eligibility

Viatical Settlements:
- Sell policy if terminally ill (2 years or less)
- Receive 50-80% of death benefit
- Proceeds generally tax-free
- Third party becomes beneficiary

1035 Exchange:
- Exchange one life insurance policy for another
- Tax-free transfer of cash value
- Must follow strict rules
- Use to upgrade to better policy

Retirement Income Strategies:

Overfunding Strategy:
- Pay more premiums than necessary (universal life)
- Build substantial cash value
- Tax-free loans in retirement
- Tax-free death benefit to heirs
- Complex, requires expert guidance
- Better for high earners maxing retirement accounts

Pension Maximization:
- Take higher pension payout (life only, no survivor benefit)
- Use savings to buy life insurance
- If you die first, spouse receives death benefit
- Risky strategy, needs careful analysis

Business Uses:

Key Person Insurance:
- Company insures key employee
- Death benefit compensates company for loss
- Funds to recruit/train replacement
- Covers lost revenue

Buy-Sell Agreement Funding:
- Partners buy insurance on each other
- If partner dies, death benefit buys deceased's share
- Provides liquidity for purchase
- Ensures smooth business transition

Supplemental Executive Retirement Plan (SERP):
- Company buys policy on executive
- Tax-deferred cash value growth
- Executive receives retirement benefits
- Golden handcuffs for retention

Estate Planning Uses:

Estate Tax Liquidity:
- Federal estate tax: 40% over $13.61 million (2026) per individual
- Life insurance provides cash to pay taxes
- Prevents forced sale of assets

Wealth Equalization:
- Leave business to one child
- Life insurance death benefit to other child
- Fair inheritance distribution

Charitable Giving:
- Name charity as beneficiary
- Significant donation from modest premiums
- Estate tax deduction

Dynasty Trust:
- Irrevocable trust owns policy
- Provides for multiple generations
- Avoids estate taxes
- Keeps wealth in family

SECTION 10: COMMON MISTAKES AND TIPS
================================================================================

Mistakes to Avoid:

1. Buying Whole Life Too Early:
   - Young families need more coverage
   - Term provides 10x more coverage for same premium
   - Buy term, invest the difference
   - Consider permanent later when established

2. Insufficient Coverage:
   - Average coverage: $178,000 (LIMRA)
   - Average needs: $500K-$1M+
   - Leaves family underprotected

3. Forgetting to Update Beneficiaries:
   - Ex-spouse still named
   - Deceased beneficiary
   - Minor children (needs trust)

4. Letting Term Policy Lapse:
   - Miss opportunity to convert
   - Lose coverage when need it most
   - Plan for term conversion or renewal

5. Buying Insurance from Bank/Car Dealer:
   - Credit life insurance overpriced
   - Better options available
   - High commissions built in

6. Not Shopping Around:
   - Premiums vary 30-50% between companies
   - Each insurer has different underwriting
   - Get 3-5 quotes minimum

7. Lying on Application:
   - Claim can be denied (first 2 years)
   - Contestability period
   - Be truthful, better than denial

8. Buying Too Many Riders:
   - Adds unnecessary cost
   - Stick to essential: waiver of premium, accelerated death benefit
   - Skip gimmicks

9. Using Insurance as Primary Investment:
   - Cash value grows slowly
   - High fees
   - Better investment options (401k, IRA, taxable accounts)
   - Insurance is for protection first

10. Not Reading Policy:
    - Understand what you bought
    - Review during free look period
    - Ask questions

Tips for Buying Life Insurance:

1. Buy Young and Healthy:
   - Premiums lock in at age purchased
   - 35-year-old pays half what 45-year-old pays
   - Don't wait

2. Start with Term:
   - Most affordable
   - Get adequate coverage first
   - Add permanent later if needed

3. Calculate Needs Carefully:
   - Don't guess
   - Use DIME formula or similar
   - Consider future needs (kids' college)

4. Shop Independent Brokers:
   - Access to multiple insurers
   - Find best rate class for your situation
   - No cost to you

5. Get Quotes from Multiple Insurers:
   - Premiums vary significantly
   - Each company underwrites differently
   - One may be better for your health situation

6. Consider Convertibility:
   - Valuable option for term policies
   - If health declines, can convert to permanent
   - No medical exam required

7. Bundle Savings:
   - Some insurers discount for multiple policies
   - Spouse policies together
   - Ask about discounts

8. Annual vs Monthly Premiums:
   - Annual saves 3-5%
   - Monthly convenience fee
   - Consider if budget allows

9. Review Every 3-5 Years:
   - Life changes
   - Coverage needs change
   - May be able to decrease or increase

10. Work with Licensed Agent/Advisor:
    - Complex product
    - Professional guidance valuable
    - Ensure proper coverage

Red Flags:

- Agent pushing whole life for young family (term usually better)
- "Investment returns" emphasis (insurance is protection first)
- High-pressure sales tactics
- Unclear fees and expenses
- Guaranteed returns claims (no such thing)
- "No medical exam" as main selling point (may cost more)
- Replacing existing policy without clear benefit
- Policy illustrations with unrealistic returns

Final Thoughts:

Life insurance is financial protection, not investment. Buy adequate coverage first (usually term), then consider permanent if it fits your situation. Most important: don't die without coverage if others depend on your income.

Average Annual Cost:
- Term Life: $300-$600/year ($500K, 20-year, age 35)
- Whole Life: $4,800-$7,200/year ($500K, age 35)
- Universal Life: $3,600-$6,000/year ($500K, age 35)

Remember: The best life insurance is the policy that's in force when you die. Buy coverage you can afford long-term and won't let lapse.
