RENTERS INSURANCE - COMPLETE GUIDE

================================================================================
SECTION 1: RENTERS INSURANCE FUNDAMENTALS
================================================================================

What is Renters Insurance?

Renters insurance protects your personal belongings and provides liability coverage when you rent an apartment, house, or condo. It covers your stuff, not the building itself (that's the landlord's responsibility).

Common Misconception: "My landlord's insurance covers me."
Reality: Landlord's insurance only covers the building structure, not your personal property or liability.

Why You Need It:

Personal Property Protection:
- Covers your belongings if stolen or damaged
- Average renter owns $30,000-$50,000 in possessions
- Could you afford to replace everything?

Liability Protection:
- Someone injured in your apartment
- You accidentally damage someone else's property
- Dog bites visitor
- Can protect against lawsuits

Additional Living Expenses:
- Apartment becomes uninhabitable (fire, flood)
- Hotel and food costs covered
- Maintains your standard of living

Cost: $15-$30/month average ($180-$360/year)
- One of the cheapest insurance types
- Often less than 1 dinner out per month

Shocking Statistic: Only 41% of renters have insurance (Insurance Information Institute, 2024)

SECTION 2: WHAT'S COVERED
================================================================================

Personal Property Coverage:

Standard Coverage for:

Furniture and Household Items:
- Sofas, chairs, tables
- Beds, mattresses
- Lamps, decorations
- Dishes, cookware
- Appliances you own (microwave, coffee maker)

Electronics:
- Computers and laptops
- TVs and gaming consoles
- Phones and tablets
- Cameras
- Sound systems

Clothing and Accessories:
- All your clothes
- Shoes
- Jewelry (limited, see special limits below)
- Handbags

Other Belongings:
- Books
- Sports equipment
- Musical instruments (sometimes limited)
- Bicycles
- Tools

Items NOT Owned But in Your Care:
- Borrowed items from friends
- Items you're temporarily storing for others (limitations apply)

Special Limits (Requires Additional Coverage):

Jewelry and Watches:
- Standard limit: $1,000-$2,500 total
- Single item limit: $500-$1,000
- Need scheduled personal property for expensive items

Cash and Securities:
- Cash: $200-$500 limit
- Securities/bonds: $1,000-$2,500 limit

Firearms:
- Limit: $2,000-$2,500 total

Silverware and Collectibles:
- Limit: $2,500-$5,000 total

Electronics (Some Policies):
- Per-item limit: $1,000-$2,000

Business Property:
- Very limited or excluded
- Get separate business insurance if working from home

Covered Perils (Named Perils Policy):

1. Fire or Lightning:
   - Apartment fire
   - Wildfire
   - Lightning strikes

2. Windstorm or Hail:
   - Hurricane damage
   - Tornado
   - Severe storms

3. Explosion:
   - Gas leak explosion
   - Boiler explosion

4. Riot or Civil Commotion:
   - Protest damage
   - Looting

5. Aircraft or Vehicle Damage:
   - Plane crashes into building
   - Car crashes into apartment

6. Smoke:
   - Smoke damage from fire
   - Smoke from nearby fire

7. Vandalism or Malicious Mischief:
   - Graffiti
   - Intentional property destruction

8. Theft:
   - Burglary
   - Robbery
   - Stolen items from car (often limited)

9. Volcanic Eruption:
   - Rarely relevant unless near volcanoes

10. Falling Objects:
    - Tree falls on building
    - Debris from construction

11. Weight of Ice, Snow, or Sleet:
    - Roof collapse from snow
    - Ice damage

12. Water Damage from Plumbing:
    - Burst pipes
    - Plumbing overflow
    - Does NOT cover flooding or sewer backup (separate coverage)

13. Sudden/Accidental Damage from Electrical Current:
    - Power surge
    - Electrical fire

14. Frozen Plumbing/Heating Systems:
    - If you maintain heat

Liability Coverage:

Personal Liability:
- Standard: $100,000 (minimum)
- Recommended: $300,000-$500,000
- Available: Up to $1,000,000+

Covered Situations:

Accidents in Your Home:
- Guest slips and falls
- Friend injured on faulty furniture
- Visitor trips on your clutter

Damage You Cause to Others:
- Accidentally start fire that spreads to other units
- Water leak damages neighbor's apartment
- Child breaks neighbor's property

Pet Liability:
- Dog bites visitor
- Dog damages someone's property
- Cat scratches guest requiring medical care

Note: Some breeds excluded (Pit Bulls, Rottweilers, Dobermans). Check policy.

Accidental Property Damage to Others:
- Spill wine on friend's expensive rug
- Break landlord's window
- Damage rented storage unit

Legal Defense:
- Pays legal fees if sued
- Coverage even if you're not at fault
- Settlements and judgments up to policy limit

Medical Payments to Others:

Coverage: $1,000-$5,000 (typical)

What It Covers:
- Guest injured in your apartment
- Pays medical bills regardless of fault
- No lawsuit necessary
- Covers reasonable medical expenses

Examples:
- Friend cuts hand on broken glass
- Visitor slips and sprains ankle
- Child gets minor burn on hot stove

Additional Living Expenses (Loss of Use):

Coverage: 10-40% of personal property limit

When It Applies:
- Apartment uninhabitable due to covered peril
- Fire, smoke, water damage
- Building repairs prevent occupancy
- Landlord eviction due to building damage

What's Covered:

Temporary Housing:
- Hotel or rental apartment
- Up to your normal living expenses
- Maintains standard of living

Extra Costs:
- Restaurant meals (if you normally cook)
- Pet boarding
- Storage unit rental
- Laundry costs (if you normally have in-unit laundry)

Duration: Until apartment is repaired or you find permanent housing (typically 12-24 months maximum)

Example:
- Normal rent: $1,500/month
- Temporary hotel: $2,500/month
- Insurance pays: $1,000/month difference
- Normal food: $400/month
- Restaurant food: $800/month
- Insurance pays: $400/month difference

SECTION 3: WHAT'S NOT COVERED
================================================================================

Common Exclusions:

Flooding:
- Rising water from outside
- Overflowing rivers or lakes
- Storm surge
- Need separate flood insurance (NFIP or private)

Earthquakes:
- Earthquake damage
- Separate earthquake coverage required
- Relevant in California, Pacific Northwest, other seismic zones

Sewer Backup:
- Sewage backs up into apartment
- Usually available as add-on ($40-$75/year)
- Recommended in flood-prone areas

Mold (Often):
- Mold from gradual water issues
- May be covered if from sudden covered event (burst pipe)
- Often excluded or limited

Intentional Damage:
- You intentionally damage property
- Fraud or illegal acts

Normal Wear and Tear:
- Aging appliances
- Old furniture deterioration
- Gradual depreciation

Bed Bugs and Pests:
- Infestations
- Pest control costs
- Landlord's responsibility usually

Business Property:
- Property used for business
- Need separate business policy
- Very limited coverage (often $2,500 limit on-premises, $250 off-premises)

Roommate's Property:
- Each person needs their own policy
- Your policy doesn't cover roommate's stuff

Motorized Vehicles:
- Cars, motorcycles, ATVs
- Need auto insurance
- May cover bicycle

Property in Storage (Limited):
- Usually 10% of personal property coverage
- May need to inform insurer
- Consider separate coverage for valuable items

War and Nuclear Hazard:
- Standard exclusions
- Unlikely to be relevant

High-Value Items Without Scheduling:
- Jewelry over limit
- Art and collectibles
- Expensive electronics
- Need scheduled personal property endorsement

SECTION 4: COVERAGE TYPES AND AMOUNTS
================================================================================

Personal Property Coverage Levels:

How Much Coverage Do You Need?

Home Inventory Method:
1. Go room by room
2. List all belongings
3. Estimate replacement cost
4. Add up total

Average by Living Situation:
- College student: $10,000-$20,000
- Young professional: $20,000-$40,000
- Established renter: $40,000-$75,000
- Family: $50,000-$100,000+

Common Coverage Amounts:
- $15,000 (minimum, rarely sufficient)
- $25,000 (common starting point)
- $50,000 (recommended average)
- $75,000-$100,000 (comprehensive)

Cost Difference:
- $25,000 coverage: ~$15/month
- $50,000 coverage: ~$20/month
- $75,000 coverage: ~$25/month

Advice: Round up. Better to have too much than too little. Extra coverage is cheap.

Actual Cash Value (ACV) vs Replacement Cost:

Actual Cash Value (ACV):
- Pays depreciated value
- Accounts for age and wear
- Cheaper premiums (10-15% less)

Example:
- 5-year-old laptop
- Original cost: $1,000
- Current value: $300
- ACV policy pays: $300

Replacement Cost Value (RCV):
- Pays to replace with new equivalent
- No depreciation
- Higher premiums but MUCH better coverage
- Strongly recommended

Example:
- Same 5-year-old laptop
- Original cost: $1,000
- Current value: $300
- RCV policy pays: $1,000 (to buy new equivalent)

Cost Difference: RCV is only 10-15% more expensive.

Recommendation: Always choose Replacement Cost. The extra $2-5/month is worth it.

Deductible Options:

Common Deductibles:
- $250 (higher premium)
- $500 (standard, recommended)
- $1,000 (lower premium)
- $2,500 (rare, very low premium)

How It Works:
- Amount you pay before insurance kicks in
- Per occurrence/claim

Example with $500 deductible:
- Burglary loss: $3,000
- You pay: $500
- Insurance pays: $2,500

Premium Savings:
- $250 → $500 deductible: Save $30-$50/year
- $500 → $1,000 deductible: Save $50-$80/year

Recommendation: $500 deductible balances affordability with savings.

Liability Coverage Levels:

Standard Options:
- $100,000 (minimum, often required by landlord)
- $300,000 (recommended minimum)
- $500,000 (better protection)
- $1,000,000 (maximum, best value)

Cost:
- $100,000: Included in base price
- $300,000: +$2-3/month
- $500,000: +$4-5/month
- $1,000,000: +$6-8/month

Why Higher Limits Matter:
- Lawsuits can easily exceed $100,000
- Medical bills for serious injury: $50,000-$500,000+
- Legal defense costs: $10,000-$50,000+
- Protects your assets and future wages

Recommendation: Minimum $300,000, ideally $500,000. If you have assets or high income, get $1,000,000.

Umbrella Insurance:
For additional liability ($1-2 million+):
- Sits above renters policy
- Covers multiple policies (auto + renters)
- Cost: $200-$350/year for $1 million
- Consider if net worth exceeds base liability limits

Example Cost Breakdown:

- $50,000 personal property (replacement cost)
- $500 deductible
- $300,000 liability
- Base cost: $25/month ($300/year)

With Discounts:

Tips to Lower Premiums:

1. Bundle Policies:
   - Always get quotes with auto insurance together
   - Biggest discount available

2. Increase Deductible:
   - $500 → $1,000 saves $50-80/year
   - Only if you can afford higher out-of-pocket

3. Security Improvements:
   - Install smoke detectors (required anyway)
   - Add deadbolts
   - Consider security system

4. Maintain Good Credit:
   - Insurers use credit-based insurance score
   - Pay bills on time
   - Keep credit utilization low

5. Shop Around:
   - Rates vary 40-60% between companies

   - Requote every 2-3 years

6. Ask About All Discounts:

7. Pay Annually:
   - Monthly payment fees add up
   - Save 5-10% paying yearly

8. Stay Claims-Free:
   - Don't file small claims
   - Pay out of pocket for minor losses under $1,000-$1,500
   - Saves on premiums long-term

SECTION 6: FILING A CLAIM
================================================================================

When to File a Claim:

File for:
- Major losses over $2,000-$3,000
- Liability incidents
- Total loss (fire, major theft)
- Losses significantly exceeding deductible

Consider NOT Filing for:
- Small losses under $1,000
- Claims near deductible amount
- Multiple small claims (increases rates)

Why? Filing claims can:
- Increase premiums 10-40%
- Lead to non-renewal
- Follow you to new insurers (CLUE report)

Rule of Thumb: File claims only if loss exceeds deductible by $1,000+

Claim Process:

Step 1 - Immediate Actions (Within 24 Hours):

For Theft:
- Call police immediately
- File police report (required for claim)
- Get report number
- Don't touch/disturb scene

For Fire/Water Damage:
- Ensure safety first
- Call 911 if needed
- Document damage (photos/videos)
- Prevent further damage (tarp over hole, etc.)
- Save receipts for emergency expenses

For Liability:
- Get injured person medical help
- Don't admit fault
- Exchange contact information
- Document scene
- Notify insurer immediately

Step 2 - Contact Insurance Company (Within 24-48 Hours):

Information to Provide:
- Policy number
- Date and time of incident
- Location
- Description of what happened
- Police report number (if theft/vandalism)
- Photos and videos
- List of damaged/stolen items

Methods:
- Phone (fastest): Call claims number on policy
- Online: Submit through insurer's website/app
- Agent: Contact your agent

Step 3 - Document Everything:

Create Detailed Inventory:
- Item description
- Brand and model
- Purchase date
- Original cost
- Current condition
- Photos if available

Supporting Documentation:
- Receipts
- Credit card statements
- Warranty documents
- Owner's manuals
- Photos from before incident

Tips:
- More detail = faster claim
- Don't exaggerate (fraud)
- Be honest and thorough

Step 4 - Meet with Adjuster:

What to Expect:
- Adjuster inspects damage
- May take photos
- Asks questions about incident
- Reviews your documentation

Preparation:
- Have documentation ready
- Point out all damage
- Be available and cooperative
- Ask questions if unclear

Timeline: Usually within 7-10 days of filing

Step 5 - Review Settlement Offer:

Adjuster Determines:
- Covered items
- Replacement/repair costs
- Depreciation (if ACV policy)
- Total payout minus deductible

You Receive:
- Settlement letter
- Explanation of calculation
- Payment timeline

If You Disagree:
- Request explanation
- Provide additional documentation
- Negotiate (yes, you can!)
- Request re-inspection
- File complaint with state insurance department

Step 6 - Receive Payment:

Timelines by State:
- Most states: 30-60 days
- Faster for straightforward claims
- Longer for complex claims

Payment Methods:
- Direct deposit
- Check mailed to you
- Check to you and landlord (if liability)

Replacement Cost Policies:
- Initial payment: ACV (depreciated value)
- Final payment: Additional amount after you replace items with receipts

Claim Settlement Examples:

Example 1 - Burglary:
- Loss: $5,000 (laptop, TV, jewelry, clothes)
- Deductible: $500
- Policy: Replacement cost
- Settlement: $4,500
- Timeline: 14 days

Example 2 - Fire Damage:
- Personal property loss: $30,000
- Additional living expenses: $6,000 (3 months hotel)
- Deductible: $1,000
- Policy: Replacement cost
- Settlement: $35,000
- Timeline: 45 days

Example 3 - Water Damage (Burst Pipe):
- Furniture and electronics damaged: $8,000
- Deductible: $500
- Policy: ACV
- Depreciation: -$2,500
- Settlement: $5,000
- Timeline: 21 days

Example 4 - Liability (Guest Injury):
- Medical bills: $25,000
- Legal fees: $10,000
- Liability limit: $300,000
- Your deductible: $0 (liability has no deductible)
- Settlement: $35,000 (paid by insurance)

Common Claim Mistakes:

1. Not Having Home Inventory:
   - Can't remember what you owned
   - Can't prove ownership
   - Underpaid settlements

2. Waiting Too Long:
   - Report immediately
   - Most policies require prompt reporting

3. Throwing Away Damaged Items:
   - Keep until adjuster inspects
   - Need for proof

4. Exaggerating Loss:
   - Insurance fraud
   - Claim denied
   - Policy cancelled

5. Not Reading Settlement:
   - Don't understand what's covered
   - Miss opportunity to negotiate

6. Accepting First Offer Without Review:
   - May be low
   - Can negotiate
   - Get second opinion

SECTION 7: REQUIRED COVERAGE AND LANDLORD REQUIREMENTS
================================================================================

Landlord Requirements:

Many Landlords Require Renters Insurance:

Typical Requirements:
- Minimum $100,000 personal liability
- $300,000 preferred by some landlords
- Landlord named as "interested party" or "additional interest"

Why Landlords Require It:
- Protects tenant's belongings (fewer complaints)
- Liability coverage protects landlord indirectly
- Reduces tenant lawsuits against landlord
- Professional risk management

Lease Language:
"Tenant shall maintain renters insurance with minimum $100,000 liability coverage and name Landlord as additional interest."

Legal? Yes, in most states landlords can require renters insurance.

Can Landlord Require Specific Amount?
Yes, typically up to $100,000-$500,000 liability. Must be reasonable.

Proof of Insurance:

What Landlords Need:
- Declaration page (Dec page)
- Shows coverage amounts
- Policy effective dates
- Landlord listed as interested party

When to Provide:
- Before move-in
- At lease renewal
- If policy changes
- If switching insurers

What Happens If You Don't Get It:
- Lease violation
- Fees or fines
- Forced coverage (landlord buys policy, charges you inflated price)
- Eviction (rare but possible)

Additional Insured vs Additional Interest:

Additional Interest (Standard):
- Landlord receives notifications
- Policy changes
- Cancellation notices
- No coverage provided to landlord

Additional Insured (Rare):
- Landlord actually covered under your policy
- Generally not necessary
- May increase premium
- Most landlords only need "additional interest"

State Laws:

States Allowing Mandatory Renters Insurance:
- Most states (45+)
- Can require as lease condition

States with Restrictions:
- Oklahoma: Landlord cannot require specific insurer
- Wisconsin: Landlord must allow proof of equivalent coverage

Check local laws if uncertain.

SECTION 8: COVERAGE EXTENSIONS AND ENDORSEMENTS
================================================================================

Optional Coverage Add-Ons:

Scheduled Personal Property (Most Common):

What It Is:
- Additional coverage for high-value items
- Items "scheduled" (listed individually)
- Full replacement cost
- No deductible
- Broader coverage (includes mysterious disappearance)

When You Need It:
- Engagement ring over $5,000
- Expensive jewelry
- Fine art
- Musical instruments
- Camera equipment
- Collectibles
- Antiques

Cost:
- $10-$30 per $1,000 value annually
- Example: $10,000 engagement ring = $100-$300/year

Process:
- Get appraisal
- Submit to insurer
- Added to policy

Water Backup Coverage:

Covers:
- Sewer backup
- Sump pump failure
- Drain backup

Limits:
- $5,000-$25,000 typical

Cost: $40-$100/year

Recommended For:
- Basement apartments
- Areas with old plumbing
- Flood-prone areas

Earthquake Coverage:

Necessary In:
- California
- Pacific Northwest
- Alaska
- Other seismic zones

Cost:
- California: $100-$300/year for renters
- Other areas: $50-$150/year

Deductibles:
- Typically 10-20% of coverage
- High deductibles keep premiums low

Flood Insurance:

Through NFIP (National Flood Insurance Program):
- Contents coverage up to $100,000
- Separate from renters policy
- Must wait 30 days after purchase for coverage to begin

Cost: $300-$700/year (varies by flood zone)

When You Need It:
- Flood zone (A or V)
- First floor apartment
- Near water
- Area with flood history

Note: Renters insurance does NOT cover flooding. Separate policy required.

Identity Theft Coverage:

Coverage:
- Legal fees
- Lost wages
- Credit monitoring
- Document replacement

Limits: $10,000-$25,000

Cost: $25-$50/year or included free

Value: Moderate. Free credit monitoring may be sufficient.

Pet Damage Coverage:

Covers:
- Damage your pet causes to rental property
- May be required by landlord if you have pet
- Separate from liability (which covers injury to others)

Cost: $10-$25/month

Limits: $500-$5,000

Business Property Coverage:

Coverage:
- Property used for business
- Home office equipment
- Business inventory
- Business liability (limited)

Limits: $5,000-$10,000 typical

Cost: $50-$150/year

Better Option: Separate business owner's policy (BOP) if significant business property.

Valuable Items Coverage (Blanket):

Alternative to scheduled items:
- Increases limits for categories
- Example: Jewelry limit from $1,500 to $5,000
- Less paperwork than scheduling
- Still has per-item limits

Cost: $50-$150/year

Replacement Cost for Electronics:

Some Policies:
- Electronics covered at ACV only
- Can add RCV endorsement
- Worth it if you have expensive electronics

Cost: +$2-$5/month

Credit Card Coverage:

Covers:
- Unauthorized use of credit cards
- Stolen checks
- Counterfeit currency

Limits: $500-$1,000

Value: Low. Credit cards already have fraud protection.

SECTION 9: CLAIMS SCENARIOS AND EXAMPLES
================================================================================

Real-Life Claim Scenarios:

Scenario 1 - Apartment Fire:

Situation:
- Kitchen fire destroys apartment
- All belongings lost
- Displaced for 4 months

Coverage:
- Personal property: $50,000
- Loss of use: $20,000 (40% of personal property)
- Liability: $300,000

Costs:
- Replace belongings: $47,000
- Temporary housing: $8,000 (4 months × $2,000/month)
- Restaurant meals: $3,000
- Total: $58,000

Insurance Pays:
- Belongings: $46,500 ($47,000 - $500 deductible)
- Housing: $8,000 (no deductible on loss of use)
- Meals: $3,000
- Total paid: $57,500

Out-of-Pocket: $500 (deductible)

Scenario 2 - Burglary:

Situation:
- Apartment broken into
- Electronics, jewelry, and cash stolen

Items Stolen:
- Laptop: $1,200
- TV: $800
- Gaming console: $500
- Jewelry: $3,500
- Cash: $400
- Total value: $6,400

Coverage Limits:
- Electronics: Covered
- Jewelry: $1,500 limit (need scheduled property for more)
- Cash: $200 limit
- Total covered: $4,000

Insurance Pays:
- Laptop: $1,200
- TV: $800
- Gaming console: $500
- Jewelry: $1,500 (limit)
- Cash: $200 (limit)
- Total: $4,200
- Minus deductible: $3,700

Out-of-Pocket:
- $500 (deductible)
- $2,200 (jewelry and cash above limits)
- Total: $2,700

Lesson: Schedule high-value jewelry separately.

Scenario 3 - Liability Claim (Guest Injury):

Situation:
- Friend trips on rug
- Falls and breaks arm
- Sues for medical bills and pain/suffering

Costs:
- Emergency room: $3,500
- Surgery: $15,000
- Physical therapy: $2,500
- Lost wages: $5,000
- Pain and suffering: $25,000
- Legal fees: $10,000
- Total lawsuit: $61,000

Coverage:
- Personal liability: $300,000
- Medical payments: $5,000

Insurance Pays:
- Medical payments: $5,000 (paid immediately, no lawsuit needed)
- Settlement: $56,000 (after negotiation)
- Legal defense: $10,000
- Total: $71,000

Your Cost: $0 (liability coverage has no deductible)

Without Insurance: $71,000 out of your pocket, potential bankruptcy

Scenario 4 - Water Damage to Neighbor:

Situation:
- Your washing machine hose bursts
- Water floods your apartment and neighbor's below
- Neighbor's furniture and electronics damaged

Your Damage:
- Your furniture: $3,000
- Your electronics: $2,000
- Total: $5,000

Neighbor's Damage:
- Furniture: $4,000
- Electronics: $3,000
- Damaged flooring: $2,500 (landlord's claim)
- Total: $9,500

Insurance Pays:
Your Property:
- $5,000 - $500 (deductible) = $4,500

Liability (Neighbor):
- $9,500 (no deductible)

Total Insurance Payout: $14,000
Your Cost: $500 (deductible)

Without Insurance: $14,500 out of pocket + potential lawsuit

Scenario 5 - Stolen Bicycle:

Situation:
- Bike stolen from apartment bike rack
- Purchase price: $1,200
- Bike is 2 years old

Coverage:
- Policy type: Actual cash value (ACV)
- Depreciation: 20% per year

Insurance Pays:
- Original cost: $1,200
- Depreciation: $480 (40% over 2 years)
- ACV: $720
- Minus deductible: $220

With Replacement Cost Coverage:
- Pays: $1,200 - $500 (deductible) = $700

Lesson: Replacement cost coverage worth the extra premium.

Scenario 6 - Dog Bite:

Situation:
- Your dog bites delivery person
- Medical treatment required

Costs:
- Emergency room: $2,500
- Follow-up treatment: $1,500
- Missed work: $1,000
- Pain and suffering claim: $10,000
- Total claim: $15,000

Coverage:
- Personal liability: $100,000
- Medical payments: $1,000

Insurance Pays:
- Medical payments: $1,000 (immediate)
- Liability settlement: $14,000
- Total: $15,000

Your Cost: $0

Potential Issue: Dog breed exclusions. Some policies exclude certain breeds (Pit Bulls, Rottweilers). If excluded, no coverage - you pay $15,000 out of pocket.

Lesson: Verify pet coverage before claim, especially for certain breeds.

SECTION 10: TIPS FOR MAXIMIZING COVERAGE
================================================================================

Create a Home Inventory:

Why It's Critical:
- Can't claim what you can't remember
- Proof of ownership
- Accurate coverage amount
- Faster claims

How to Create:

Method 1 - Room by Room:
1. Start at entrance
2. Go clockwise around room
3. List every item
4. Note brand, model, purchase date, cost
5. Take photos of everything
6. Move to next room

Method 2 - Video Walkthrough:
1. Record video of entire apartment
2. Open drawers and closets
3. Narrate what items are
4. Zoom in on high-value items
5. State purchase dates and costs if known

Method 3 - Apps:
- Sortly
- Encircle
- Know Your Stuff (NAIC)
- MyStuff (Liberty Mutual)

What to Document:

For Each Item:
- Description
- Brand and model
- Serial number

- Purchase price
- Current value
- Photos
- Receipts (if available)

High-Value Items (Extra Documentation):
- Appraisals

- Detailed descriptions

Where to Store:
- Cloud storage (Google Drive, Dropbox)

Understanding Policy Limits:

Review Your Policy:
- Personal property limit
- Liability limit
- Sublimits for specific items
- Deductible
- Loss of use percentage

Know Your Sublimits:

Common Sublimits:
- Jewelry: $1,500
- Firearms: $2,500
- Silverware: $2,500
- Cash: $200
- Securities: $1,500
- Electronics per item: $1,000-$2,000
- Business property: $2,500

If You Exceed Limits:
- Schedule valuable items separately
- Increase specific sublimits
- Get separate policy for business property

Tips for Lower Premiums:

1. Bundle with Auto: Save 10-25%
2. Increase Deductible: $500 → $1,000 saves $50-80/year
3. Security Systems: Save 5-15%
4. Stay Claims-Free: Save 5-10% after 3-5 years
5. Good Credit: Save 10-20%
6. Shop Every 2-3 Years: Find better rates
7. Pay Annually: Save 5-10%
8. Ask About Discounts: May qualify for more than offered

Avoiding Coverage Gaps:

Common Gaps:
- Between moving out and moving in (no coverage)
- Roommate's property (each needs own policy)
- High-value items above sublimits
- Flood and earthquake (need separate)
- Business property

Solutions:
- Start new policy before old ends
- Each roommate gets policy
- Schedule valuables
- Buy flood/earthquake if needed
- Get business policy if working from home
